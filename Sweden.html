Sweden, Life on Land

Sweden has around 87 billion trees, and 70 percent of its surface is covered with forests, according to Swedish Forest Industries (2024). This makes the forest industry one of Sweden’s most important business sectors. Sweden has a rich experience in managing diverse landscapes and forests efficiently through informed, effective governance. Having laws and environmental objectives addressing many areas of SDG 15 (United Nations, n.d.).

It is claimed by the Swedish Forest Industries (2024) that environmental and production goals in forestry have been balanced and given equal importance. Even though there is a one percent felling of forests annually, for every tree harvested, at least two are planted, making growth outpace the felling.

Despite these claims, Sweden experiences the most forest loss out of all the EU countries. In a study published in the journal Earth’s Future by Ahlström et al. (2022), old and previously uncut Swedish forests are cut at a steady rate of approximately 1.4 percent between the years 2003 and 2019. This number is almost equivalent to the rate stated in Swedish Forest Industries (2024), though contrastingly Ahlström et al. (2022) do not see the solution in replantation. These forests are slow-developing and have distinct structural and ecological characteristics, which means retrieving them would take centuries.

Sweden's Forest Area Over Time in Million Hectares

A chart with data from Statistics Sweden (2022) showing Sweden’s forest area over time provides another perspective for the loss of forests in Sweden. It provides the ability to compare the data from SDGs API (2025) with other data to see how it differs.
(The chart for SDGs API (2025) data can be found here: EU Countries With Forest Decrease)

The chart above shows that the change rate of forest area between each five years is both increasing and decreasing for Statistics Sweden (2022) data, with the change rate being

  • From 2000 to 2005 was an increase of 0.98 percent.
  • From 2005 to 2010 was a decrease of 6.8 percent.
  • From 2010 to 2015 was an increase of 0.14 percent.
  • From 2015 to 2020 was a decrease of 0.92 percent.

Though in total there is a significant decrease of around 2 million hectares between the years 2000 and 2020, which is a decrease of about 6.6 percent in 20 years.

Both Statistics Sweden (2022) and SDGs API (2025) show a clear decrease in forest area from the years 2000 to 2020, though Statistics Sweden (2022) shows a much higher decrease, about ten times more than SDGs API (2025), while Ahlström et al. (2022) found an even higher decrease in only old forests of about 19 percent.

It is important to note that both Statistics Sweden (2022) and SDGs API (2025) show the decrease in all forests in Sweden, while Ahlström et al. (2022) show a decrease in old, previously uncut, and unprotected forests only. Another thing to note is that Statistics Sweden (2022) and SDGs API (2025) show data from a different as well as longer time span than Ahlström et al.'s (2022) study. The cause of the forest area loss seen in both Statistics Sweden (2022) and SDGs API (2025) might not be only caused by clear-cuts; it can be caused by other factors such as natural occurrences.

Average Annual Felling Volume in Swedish Regions in Million Forest Cubic Meters (2000-2020)

Looking at the average annual felling volume of Sweden’s forests in each region gives a view on where forest loss occurs most in Sweden. The data used is from SLU Riksskogstaxeringen (2025).

The chart shows the average annual felling volume of each region in Sweden from the years 2000 until 2020. It is clear from the chart that Götaland has the biggest felling volume per year compared to the other regions, with Svealand being the second. Though when comparing the south and the north of Sweden in total, there is more felling in the northern parts.

Combining the values from the north, Upper Norrland and Lower Norrland, the average annual felling volume in Norrland is around 31.8 million forest cubic meters (m³sk), while in Götaland it is around 30.5 million forest cubic meters (m³sk). Which is a difference of around 1.3 million forest cubic meters (m³sk).

Ahlström et al.'s (2022) study shows similar results to the data from SLU Riksskogstaxeringen (2025). Stating that clear-cuts of forests are occurring across the whole region but are more prominent in the north, the reason being that most of the south's older forests were clear-cut previously by 1966.

Average Annual Forest Growth Volume in Swedish Regions in Million Forest Cubic Meters (2000-2020)

Swedish Forest Industries (2024) provides some information about tree growth in Sweden. It is said that a typical growth cycle of a forest is between 60 and 100 years. It is also said that trees in southern Sweden grow twice as much faster as trees in the northern parts. In another article by Swedish Forest Industries (2024), it is claimed that tree growth outpaces felling.

By showing data from SLU Riksskogstaxeringen (2025) in a chart of Sweden’s forest average annual volume increase in each region, the growth compared to the felling can be investigated.

The chart shows that Götaland has the biggest average annual growth volume compared to the other regions. Though comparing the north with the south, summing Upper Norrland’s and Lower Norrland’s growth volume making the north's average annual total growth volume around 53 million forest cubic meters (m³sk). The north has a higher average annual growth volume than the south, which is around 36 million forest cubic meters (m³sk).

Comparing the average annual growth volume to the average annual felling, it is clear, like Swedish Forest Industries (2024) claimed, that growth outpaces the felling. In northern Sweden the average annual growth outpaces the felling by 40 percent and in southern Sweden by 15 percent. The data from SLU Riksskogstaxeringen (2025) shows more growth in volume in the northern areas, though it does not give an insight into the growth speed of trees in each region.

According to the study by Fassl et al. (2024), Nothern Sweden contains the largest protected forests in the country. The study also shows an increased growth of the two tree species, Scots pine and downy birch, in a northern forest reserve, particularly in the recent decades. It is also noted that increasing temperatures, caused by climate change, in the northern parts have a positive impact on the species Scots pine. This could explain the significant difference in forest growth volume in the northern areas compared to the southern areas.

Percentage of Protected Forest Area Out of Total Forest Area in Swedish Regions in (2023)

A map showing the percent of protected forests in each region gives a view on how Sweden manages its forest biodiversity. The data used is from Statistics Sweden (2024).

The map shows that the region with the most protected forests is Upper Norrland, followed by Svealand. Upper Norrland has about 18.6 percent of its forest protected, while Svealand has 5.8 percent, Lower Norrland has 4.6 percent, and Götaland has 3.5 percent.

What the map is showing corresponds with Fassl et al. (2024) stating that the northern part of Sweden holds the largest protected forests. Although the size of the protected area cannot be determined by the map, Upper Norrland has the biggest proportion of protected forests.

Ahlström et al. (2022) notes that only 19.1 percent of old forests are protected, arguing that unprotected old forests may provide greater ecosystem value than those in protected zones, since they are often found in warmer and more productive areas.

Conclusion

In conclusion, Sweden is making the effort to sustain its forests and fulfill SDG 15 by planting more trees after felling. Though looking at the visualizations and the literature, there should be more measures taken to maintain Sweden’s natural forests and meet SDG 15. As suggested by Ahlström et al. (2022), protecting more forests could be beneficial in preserving and regrowing Sweden's natural forests.
