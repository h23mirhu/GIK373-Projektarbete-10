Europe and Life on Land

Within Europe, Goal 15 plays a crucial role in shaping environmental policies focused on ecological recovery and long-term sustainability. Given the continent's diverse climates and history of land use, the sustainable managements of forests is both a priority and a challenge.

Europe's Forest Area Over Time in Million Hectares

The diagram above illustrates the development of forest area, measured in millions of hectares, in Europe from 2015 to 2020, years before 2015 were not used since not all were included in the API dataset (SDGs API, 2025). The data reveals a consistent upward trend, beginning at approximately 1,015 million hectares in 2015 and increasing to nearly 1,018 million hectares by 2020.

This trend supports SDG Indicator 15.1.1, which measures the proportions of land area covered by forest and indicates that Europe is making tangible progress toward restoring its terrestrial ecosystems. The increase in forest cover also aligns with findings by Palmero-Iniesta et al. (2021), who note that 66% of Europe's forest gains between 1992 and 2015 resulted from expansion into former agricultural lands, while 34% came from regeneration in areas impacted by disturbance.

Further reinforcing this trend, Ceccherini et al. (2020) identified Europe as a region of active reforestation and tree cover recovery, particularly in temperate climates where both land availability and restoration efforts have accelerated forest regeneration. However, the same study also cautions that forest productivity and resilience vary across regions, with southern and Mediterranean zones facing water stress and lower growth potential despite increasing tree cover.

Top 5 European Countries over 10,000 Land Area with Highest Forest Area Increase from 2000 to 2020 (%)

While the overall trend in Europe shows a steady increase in forest area, national-level data reveals important regional variations. The bar chart above displays the top five European countries, with a land area over 10,000 cubic meters, and with the highest relative increase in forest area from 2000 to 2020. Bulgaria (13,31%), Italy (12,51%), and France (11,39%) lead the gains, followed by Romania (8,13%) and Spain (7,96%).

As Ceccherini et al. (2020) describe, reforestation across Europe has been particularly pronounced in temperate regions, where land availability and ecological initiatives have created favorable conditions for forest expansion. The study also notes that these gains often follow the abandonment of marginal farmland, especially in southern and central Europe.

European Countries with Forest Area Decrease from 2000 to 2020 (%)

However, as shown in the second bar chart above, not all countries are gaining forest area. Sweden (0.65%) and Finland (0.16%), despite being among the most forested countries in Europe, show minor losses. This can be attributed to commercial forestry activities, logging cycles, or temporary land-use transitions. It may also relate to climate-related limitation noted by Ceccherini et al. (2020), who emphasize that boreal and northern forests (such as those in Scandinavia), may face reduced forest productivity due to changing temperature and precipitation patterns.

These observations reinforce the need to interpret national data within climatic and ecological contexts. While some nations expand rapidly due to low initial forest cover and active policies, others may experience fluctuations due to long-term management strategies or environmental stressors. As such, achieving SDG 15.1 requires both quantitative growth and qualitative sustainability in forest management across all regions.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the diagrams provide visual evidence that Europe is progressing in its efforts to meet SDG 15, specially through natural forest regrowth. Still, regional variation in forest quality and climate-related limitations should not be overlooked when assessing the sustainability and ecological health of these forests.
